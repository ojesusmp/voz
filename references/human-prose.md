# Writing that reads as human

Removing AI tells gets you to clean. These habits get you to good. The research is honest that this half is less formally studied than the tell catalog (much of it is the inverse of the tells, plus one strong finding on surface cues). Treat it as craft, not law.

The one verified anchor: Zaitsu et al. (*PLoS ONE* 2025) found that when people judge text as AI or human, they react to surface features, phrasing, word endings, conjunctions, punctuation, far more than to deep structure. So the surface is most of the battle. Control phrasing and rhythm and you control the impression.

---

## Be specific

Specificity is the single biggest difference between human and machine prose. Models default to the general because the general is safe and average. People remember the particular.

- "The cron job corrupts the output file about once a week" beats "issues may occasionally arise with the process."
- "It saved her about three hours every Friday" beats "it delivers significant time savings."
- Name names. Use the real number. Give the actual example.

When you catch yourself writing a category word (solution, process, experience, content), ask what the specific thing is and write that instead.

## Earn the claim, do not assert it

Do not tell the reader something is important, powerful, or valuable. Show what it does and let them conclude. "Powerful" is a word the writer uses when they have not shown the power yet.

- Weak: "This is a robust, powerful feature that delivers real value."
- Earned: "It catches the duplicate before it reaches the database, so the nightly job stops failing."

## Vary the rhythm

Uniform sentence length is a tell by itself. Read your draft and look at the shape. If every sentence is fifteen to twenty words, break it up.

- A short sentence lands. Use it after a long one.
- Let one sentence run longer, carrying a fuller thought through a clause or two, so the short one after it hits harder.
- Fragments are fine. So is starting with "And" or "But". So is a one-line paragraph.

This is "burstiness": real writing has bursts of long and short. Flat, even prose feels generated.

## Cut the throat-clearing

The first sentence of a draft often only announces the real first sentence. Delete it.

- Cut: "It's important to note that the API has a rate limit." Keep: "The API allows 100 requests per minute."
- Cut openers that warm up: "When it comes to X...", "In the world of X...", "At its core, X is...".

## Prefer plain words and strong verbs

- use, not utilize. before, not prior to. help, not facilitate. about, not approximately. enough, not sufficient. start, not commence.
- Strong verbs carry sentences. "The migration drops the column" beats "the column is removed as part of the migration process."

## Write the way you would say it

The fastest human-prose test: would you say this sentence to the person, out loud, in this order? If not, rewrite it until you would. Most AI stiffness comes from sentences no human would say aloud.

## Restraint

- Do not use three adjectives where one works, or one where none is needed.
- Do not end every section with a summary of the section.
- Do not hedge a claim you are sure of. Do not over-claim one you are not.
- Leave some things unsaid. Trust the reader.

## Punctuation as rhythm, not decoration

- A comma is a short breath, a period is a full stop, a colon promises the thing after it, parentheses are an aside. Use them to control pace.
- House rule: no em dashes. Where you would reach for one, use a comma, a colon, parentheses, or split the sentence. You lose nothing.
- Curly quotes and straight quotes both read as human; consistency matters more than which.

## The point of all of it

You are not trying to fool a detector (that is a losing game, and not the goal). You are trying to write something a specific person will find clear, honest, and easy to read. Do that and it reads as human, because it is.
